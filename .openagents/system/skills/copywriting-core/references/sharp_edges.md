# Copywriting - Sharp Edges

## Feature Dump

### **Id**
feature-dump
### **Summary**
Listing features instead of explaining benefits
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  "Our product has: 256-bit encryption, real-time sync, API access..."
  Reader: "So what? Why should I care?"
  
### **Why**
  Features are what you built. Benefits are what they get.
  Users buy outcomes, not specifications. Nobody cares about your tech stack.
  
### **Solution**
  # Feature → Benefit translation:
  Feature: 256-bit encryption
  Benefit: Your data is safer than Fort Knox
  
  Feature: Real-time sync
  Benefit: Pick up exactly where you left off, on any device
  
  Feature: 99.9% uptime
  Benefit: It's there when you need it. Always.
  
  # The "So what?" test:
  For every feature, ask: "So what?"
  Keep asking until you hit the benefit.
  
  API access → "So what?"
  → Build integrations → "So what?"
  → Your tools work together seamlessly
  
  # Benefit structure:
  [Benefit headline]
  [Feature that enables it]
  [How their life is better]
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Spec sheets instead of sales pages
  - Technical jargon everywhere
  - Users ask "but what does it do for me?"
  - Low conversion despite great features
### **Detection Pattern**


## Wall Of Text

### **Id**
wall-of-text
### **Summary**
Dense paragraphs that no one reads
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  "Our comprehensive project management solution offers a wide range of
  features designed to help teams of all sizes collaborate more effectively..."
  Nobody reads that.
  
### **Why**
  Average attention span: seconds. Web reading: 20-28% of words.
  Long paragraphs: skipped entirely. People scan, they don't read.
  
### **Solution**
  1. One idea per paragraph
     Break after every thought.
  
  2. Maximum 3-4 lines per paragraph
     On mobile, that's 2 sentences max.
  
  3. Use structure
     Headlines break up content
     Bullets make lists scannable
     Bold highlights key phrases
  
  4. The F-pattern
     Top line: Most important
     Left side: Key words
  
  5. Inverted pyramid
     Lead with the conclusion
     Details come after
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Low time on page
  - High scroll abandonment
  - Users skim to CTA
  - \'"TL;DR" comments\'
### **Detection Pattern**


## Clever Curse

### **Id**
clever-curse
### **Summary**
Prioritizing cleverness over clarity
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  "Synergize your workflow symphony"
  "Where data dances with destiny"
  Writer thinks: "That's creative!"
  Reader thinks: "What does this even mean?"
  
### **Why**
  Clever copy makes the writer feel smart.
  Clear copy makes the reader feel smart.
  Confused readers don't convert.
  
### **Solution**
  1. Say what you mean
     Clever: "Orchestrate your operational opus"
     Clear: "Get your team on the same page"
  
  2. The mom test
     Would your mom understand this?
     If not, simplify.
  
  3. The 5-second test
     Show someone for 5 seconds
     They should understand the offering
  
  # Clarity hierarchy:
  1. Crystal clear
  2. Clear and interesting
  3. Never just clever
  
  # Good clear copy:
  Slack: "Where work happens"
  Stripe: "Payments infrastructure for the internet"
  Mailchimp: "Send better email"
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Users confused about what you do
  - Low 5-second test scores
  - \'"Sounds cool but..." feedback\'
  - High bounce rate on landing pages
### **Detection Pattern**


## Me Monster

### **Id**
me-monster
### **Summary**
Copy that talks about the company instead of the customer
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  "We are the leading provider of..."
  "Our company was founded in..."
  "We're passionate about..."
  Every sentence starts with us.
  
### **Why**
  Nobody cares about you. They care about themselves.
  Readers ask: "What's in it for me?" not "What's your company story?"
  
### **Solution**
  1. The you/we ratio
     Count "you" vs "we/our"
     You should appear 2-3x more
  
  2. Flip the perspective
     We: "We built the fastest..."
     You: "You'll never wait for..."
  
  3. Start with them
     Not: "We help teams collaborate"
     But: "Your team can finally stop searching through emails"
  
  # Perspective shift examples:
  We: "We offer 24/7 support"
  You: "Get help whenever you need it"
  
  We: "We've helped 10,000 customers"
  You: "Join 10,000 teams who work smarter"
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Every sentence starts with we/our
  - About page content on homepage
  - Self-congratulatory tone
  - Users don't see themselves in copy
### **Detection Pattern**
We are|We offer|Our team|We believe|We help

## Jargon Jungle

### **Id**
jargon-jungle
### **Summary**
Using industry jargon readers don't understand
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  "Our AI-powered SaaS solution leverages machine learning to optimize
  your KPIs through automated workflows and robust API integrations."
  Technical reader: "What does it actually do?"
  Everyone else: Leaves.
  
### **Why**
  Jargon is a crutch. It signals insider status to insiders.
  It excludes everyone else. Confusion creates friction.
  
### **Solution**
  # Plain language alternatives:
  Leverage → Use
  Robust → Strong
  Innovative → New
  Seamless → Easy
  Best-in-class → [Delete this]
  Synergy → [Delete this too]
  
  # The explain-to-a-friend test:
  Say it out loud to a friend.
  If you use different words, use those.
  
  # Jargon decoder:
  "Seamless integration" → "Works with your tools"
  "End-to-end solution" → "Everything you need"
  "Scalable platform" → "Grows with you"
  "Leverage" → "Use"
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Non-technical buyers bounce
  - Sales has to "translate"
  - Different copy for different audiences
  - Sounds like everyone else
### **Detection Pattern**
leverage|utilize|synergy|best-in-class|end-to-end|robust

## Vague Value Prop

### **Id**
vague-value-prop
### **Summary**
Generic claims that could apply to anyone
### **Severity**
critical
### **Situation**
  "We help businesses grow"
  "The best solution for teams"
  "Making work easier"
  Could be any company. Therefore, it's no company.
  
### **Why**
  Generic value props blend into the noise.
  Specific value props stand out.
  Specificity builds credibility.
  
### **Solution**
  1. Get specific
     Generic: "Grow your business"
     Specific: "Get 30% more leads from your existing traffic"
  
  2. Name the pain
     Generic: "Better project management"
     Specific: "Stop losing tasks in email"
  
  3. Quantify when possible
     "Faster" → "3x faster"
     "Save money" → "Save $500/month"
  
  4. Own a word
     Volvo: Safety
     Apple: Simplicity
     What's yours?
  
  # Value prop template:
  For [specific audience] who [specific problem],
  [Product] is the [category] that [specific benefit].
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Interchangeable with competitors
  - No memorable positioning
  - We do a lot of things
  - Weak differentiation
### **Detection Pattern**


## Weak Cta

### **Id**
weak-cta
### **Summary**
Calls-to-action that don't compel action
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  "Submit" "Click here" "Learn more"
  Generic, boring, no urgency. CTA is where conversion happens.
  
### **Why**
  CTAs are where conversion happens.
  Weak CTA = weak conversion.
  CTA should tell them what they get.
  
### **Solution**
  1. Value-driven CTAs
     Not: "Submit"
     But: "Get my free guide"
  
     Not: "Learn more"
     But: "See how it works"
  
  2. First person works
     "Start my trial" > "Start your trial"
  
  3. Reduce friction in CTA
     "Buy now" → High commitment
     "See pricing" → Lower commitment
     "Get started free" → Even lower
  
  4. Action + benefit
     Action: "Download"
     Action + benefit: "Download the playbook"
  
  # CTA hierarchy (by commitment):
  Low: Learn more / See how it works
  Medium: Try it free / Start free trial
  High: Buy now / Add to cart
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Low click-through rates
  - Users unsure what happens next
  - Generic button text everywhere
  - No A/B testing on CTAs
### **Detection Pattern**
>Submit<|>Click here<|>Learn more<

## Missing Specifics

### **Id**
missing-specifics
### **Summary**
Abstract claims without concrete proof
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  "Trusted by thousands" "Saves you time" "Better results"
  None of this is believable because none of it is specific.
  
### **Why**
  Specifics are believable. Generalities are ignored.
  "Thousands" sounds made up. "10,847 teams" sounds real.
  
### **Solution**
  1. Use real numbers
     Not: "Saves time"
     But: "Saves 4.5 hours per week"
  
  2. Name names
     Not: "Trusted by leading companies"
     But: "Trusted by Stripe, Shopify, and Notion"
  
  3. Specific outcomes
     Not: "Better results"
     But: "23% higher conversion rate"
  
  4. Concrete examples
     Not: "Flexible"
     But: "Works with Slack, Notion, Asana, and 100+ tools"
  
  # Specificity example:
  Vague: "High uptime"
  Specific: "99.99% uptime. That's 4 minutes of downtime per month."
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Claims without proof
  - Round numbers everywhere
  - No case studies or data
  - \'"Take our word for it" vibe\'
### **Detection Pattern**


## One Size Fits All

### **Id**
one-size-fits-all
### **Summary**
Same copy for every audience and stage
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  Landing page = Homepage copy
  Email = Landing page copy
  One piece of copy pasted everywhere.
  
### **Why**
  Different people need different messages.
  Awareness stage needs problem recognition.
  Decision stage needs proof and action.
  
### **Solution**
  # Match copy to stage:
  AWARENESS:
  "Struggling with [problem]?"
  Focus: Problem recognition
  
  CONSIDERATION:
  "Here's how we solve [problem]"
  Focus: Solution education
  
  DECISION:
  "Start your free trial today"
  Focus: Action and proof
  
  # Match copy to channel:
  AD: Hook + promise (few words)
  LANDING PAGE: Full value prop + proof
  EMAIL: Personal, conversational
  PRODUCT: Clear, helpful, brief
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Same message everywhere
  - Low conversion across stages
  - Generic rather than targeted
  - Not speaking to where they are
### **Detection Pattern**


## Passive Voice Trap

### **Id**
passive-voice-trap
### **Summary**
Passive construction that weakens copy
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  "Your project will be completed faster"
  "Tasks are organized automatically"
  Who's doing what? Unclear. Energy? None.
  
### **Why**
  Active voice is direct and powerful.
  Passive voice hides the actor and bores readers.
  
### **Solution**
  1. Find the actor
     Passive: "Reports are generated automatically"
     Active: "Generate reports automatically"
  
  2. Use action verbs
     Passive: "Your data is protected"
     Active: "We protect your data"
     Better: "You control your data"
  
  3. Lead with action
     Passive: "Results can be seen in the dashboard"
     Active: "See results in your dashboard"
  
  # "By" is a warning sign
  "Emails are sent by the system"
  → "The system sends emails"
  → "Send emails automatically"
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Weak, indirect sentences
  - Unclear who does what
  - Low energy copy
  - \'"By" appears frequently\'
### **Detection Pattern**
is being|are being|will be|was done|were made

## Fear Of White Space

### **Id**
fear-of-white-space
### **Summary**
Cramming too much copy into limited space
### **Severity**
medium
### **Situation**
  Hero section with: long headline, longer subheadline,
  three paragraphs, four bullets, two CTAs, trust badges,
  video, form. Nobody can process this.
  
### **Why**
  White space is not wasted space. It focuses attention.
  Cluttered = overwhelming. Readers skip cluttered sections.
  
### **Solution**
  1. One message per section
     Hero: Main value prop only
     Features: Benefits only
     Proof: Testimonials only
  
  2. Prioritize ruthlessly
     What's the ONE thing they need to know?
     Say that. Cut the rest.
  
  3. Progressive disclosure
     Surface level: Key message
     Deeper: More detail (if they want)
  
  4. Let copy breathe
     Short paragraphs
     Plenty of margin
     Strategic whitespace
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Overwhelming first impression
  - Users scroll past without reading
  - Important messages buried
  - Cluttered design
### **Detection Pattern**


## Ignored Error States

### **Id**
ignored-error-states
### **Summary**
Treating error messages as an afterthought
### **Severity**
high
### **Situation**
  "Error 422: Unprocessable entity"
  "Invalid input"
  "Something went wrong"
  User is frustrated. This makes it worse.
  
### **Why**
  Errors happen at vulnerable moments. Users are already frustrated.
  Your error message can help or hurt. Bad copy makes it worse.
  
### **Solution**
  1. Be human
     Not: "Error 404"
     But: "Hmm, we couldn't find that page."
  
  2. Explain what happened
     Not: "Invalid input"
     But: "That email doesn't look right.
           Make sure it has an @ symbol."
  
  3. Tell them what to do
     Not: "Form submission failed"
     But: "Try again, or contact us at help@..."
  
  4. Take responsibility
     Not: "You entered wrong data"
     But: "Something went wrong on our end"
  
  # Error message structure:
  [What happened - in human terms]
  [Why it might have happened]
  [What they can do now]
  
### **Symptoms**
  - Technical error codes shown
  - Users stuck at errors
  - No guidance on next steps
  - Higher support tickets
### **Detection Pattern**
Error \d+|Invalid|Something went wrong
